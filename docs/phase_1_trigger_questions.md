Below is a **structured diagnostic question set** you can actually use in client conversations.
The goal is not to “chat about processes” but to **systematically expose automation potential** across all 5 categories.

The key difference:
Each question is designed to trigger **specific signals** (repetition, fragmentation, etc.), not vague discussion.

---

# 1. Hidden repetition (find the time sinks)

### Core questions

* What tasks do you or your team perform every single day?
* Which tasks follow the exact same steps every time?
* What did you personally do yesterday that felt repetitive?
* Which tasks would you assign to a junior or intern without much thinking?

### Follow-up probes

* How many times per day/week does this happen?
* How long does one instance take?
* What tools are involved in each step?

### What you’re looking for

* copy-paste loops
* manual data transfers
* repeated file handling
* recurring reporting

---

### Example conversation outcome

Client says:

> “We process incoming orders from email.”

You dig:

* “How many per day?” → 80
* “Steps?” → open → read → type → confirm

→ You’ve found a high-value repetition case.

---

# 2. Process fragmentation (human as connector)

### Core questions

* Which processes involve multiple tools or systems?
* Where do employees manually transfer data between systems?
* Where do you export and re-import data?
* Which tools don’t “talk to each other”?

### Follow-up probes

* What exactly is copied from A to B?
* What happens if someone forgets a step?
* Is there a “main system” or is everything scattered?

### What you’re looking for

* Excel as a bridge
* email as transport layer
* duplicated data across tools

---

### Example conversation outcome

Client says:

> “We take data from CRM and upload it into our email tool.”

→ classic fragmentation
→ strong automation candidate

---

# 3. Decision bottlenecks (AI / rule-based opportunities)

### Core questions

* Where does someone need to “check” or “decide” something?
* Which tasks require reviewing emails, documents, or requests?
* Where do experienced employees make quick judgment calls?
* What gets escalated because “someone needs to look at it”?

### Follow-up probes

* What criteria are used for decisions?
* Are there common patterns?
* How often are decisions obvious vs. unclear?

### What you’re looking for

* classification tasks
* routing decisions
* prioritization
* filtering

---

### Example conversation outcome

Client says:

> “We read every customer email and decide who handles it.”

→ this is not pure human judgment
→ it’s structured classification in disguise

---

# 4. Error-prone steps (where mistakes cost money)

### Core questions

* Where do mistakes happen most often?
* Which steps require double-checking?
* Where do you manually type or transfer data?
* What causes customer complaints or corrections?

### Follow-up probes

* What kind of errors occur?
* How often do they happen?
* What’s the impact when they happen?

### What you’re looking for

* manual data entry
* mismatches between systems
* inconsistent formats

---

### Example conversation outcome

Client says:

> “Sometimes wrong addresses are entered into the system.”

→ manual entry problem
→ automation + validation opportunity

---

# 5. Latency (where work is waiting)

### Core questions

* Where do processes get stuck or delayed?
* What are you waiting for most of the time?
* Which tasks sit in inboxes before being processed?
* Where do approvals slow things down?

### Follow-up probes

* How long does each step take vs. waiting time?
* Who is responsible at each stage?
* Is there visibility into the current status?

### What you’re looking for

* idle time between steps
* approval bottlenecks
* missing triggers

---

### Example conversation outcome

Client says:

> “Orders wait for approval before processing.”

→ latency problem
→ potential for automation or pre-validation

---

# How to use this in practice (important)

Do NOT run through this like a checklist.

Instead:

### Step 1: Start broad

Ask:

* “Walk me through how an order is processed from start to finish.”

### Step 2: Zoom in with targeted questions

Use the categories naturally:

* repetition → “How often does this happen?”
* fragmentation → “Where does the data go next?”
* decisions → “Who decides this?”
* errors → “What goes wrong here?”
* latency → “Does it ever sit somewhere?”

---

# A real example flow (order automation)

Client describes process.

You extract:

* repetition → 100 orders/day
* fragmentation → email → ERP → shipping tool
* decisions → “we check if order is valid”
* errors → wrong product codes
* latency → waits for approval

You now have:
→ 5 independent reasons to automate one process

---

# One critical point

If you only ask:

> “What could be automated?”

You’ll get weak answers.

If you ask:

* “What do you repeat?”
* “Where do things break?”
* “Where do you wait?”
* “What do you check?”

You get real insights.
